## Topic 1: Tashkent Stock Exchange Development Since 2020

### Key Facts and Timeline

The Republican Stock Exchange "Toshkent" (UZSE) was founded on 8 April 1994 as a state-owned open joint stock company, initially created to facilitate the privatization of Soviet-era enterprises. It remains the **only equities and corporate bonds trading platform in Uzbekistan**. As of 2016, Korea Exchange (KRX) holds a 25%-minus-one-share stake in exchange for providing a unified IT trading infrastructure; the government retains 75%-plus-one-share.

**Pre-2020 baseline:** The market operated in relative obscurity. The first-ever IPO on the exchange occurred on 11 April 2018 (Kvarts). The brokerage and asset management ecosystem was thin, and international investor access was effectively nonexistent.

**2020 — Reform decree:** President Shavkat Mirziyoyev approved a comprehensive 2020 capital market reform package directing the consolidation of all capital market legislation into a single code and setting a target to increase the market's free float to **10% of GDP by 2025**. This marked the beginning of a sustained policy-driven push to develop the market.

**2022 — EBRD record investment:** The EBRD invested a record USD 900 million in 26 projects in Uzbekistan, making it the leading EBRD recipient in Central Asia for the third consecutive year, with financial sector development explicitly targeted.

**2023 — Structural acceleration:**
- Presidential Decree № PD-291 embedded a capital markets "Roadmap" for 2023–2025 as official policy.
- Capital market regulation was transferred to a new body — the **National Agency of Perspective Projects (NAPP)** — in September 2023, replacing the previous regulator.
- A "Regulatory Sandbox" regime was established to support fintech and market infrastructure development.
- In October 2023 alone, trading volumes on UZSE reached UZS 20.7 billion (~USD 1.7 million), an **8.5-fold increase** compared to the same period in 2022, coinciding with the highest UZLI score in the nine-year data series (76.41, recorded 20 December 2023).

**2024:**
- Total trading volume on UZSE reached approximately **USD 2 billion**, a 7.5-fold increase over the prior year.
- The UCI (Uzbekistan Composite Index) declined by **5.6%** in the second half of 2024, attributed to low market liquidity and declining investor confidence in state-owned enterprises including Uzmetkombinat and Uzbektelecom.
- A Central Securities Depository is being established (phased from 2025), a prerequisite for international settlement interoperability.

**2025:**
- Total stock trades: **8.078 trillion soums** across **362,420 transactions**; 33% increase in Main Board volume over 2024.
- UZSE reported a **43% increase in net profit** for 2025.
- Market capitalization reached **USD 23.67 billion** (December 2025).
- GDP growth: **7.7%** in 2025 (exceeding earlier projections).

**2026 (YTD):**
- Market cap as of January 2026 (listed shares only): **USD 9.7 billion**; including OTC: approximately 677 additional companies and ~862 total securities admitted.
- UCI Index: 890.5 points in April 2026.
- UzNIF dual listing on TSE and LSE completed (May 2026) — see Topic 2.
- Plans announced for a platform enabling trading of non-resident securities (Tesla, Google, etc.).

## Topic 2: UzNIF IPO Significance

### What Is UzNIF?

The **National Investment Fund of the Republic of Uzbekistan (UzNIF)** was established in 2024 under the management of the Ministry of Economy and Finance. It functions as a sovereign holding vehicle, managing government stakes in **18 key state-owned enterprises** across utilities, energy production, telecommunications, banking, and transport. Shareholding stakes range from 20% to 40% per company. Holdings include Uzbekistan Airways and Uzbekistan Airports, among others.

UzNIF was designed as a structured privatization instrument: rather than wholesale denationalization, the government offers **sizable minority stakes** in SOEs via public capital markets, retaining strategic control while unlocking market pricing and institutional ownership.

### Historical Significance

- **First international IPO in Uzbekistan's history.**
- Largest IPO on the London Stock Exchange in 2026.
- Largest closed-end fund listing on LSE since 2021.

- The transaction closed within six weeks of Uzbekistan completing its first international bond market transaction — two international capital markets milestones in rapid succession, described by analysts as *"a genuine inflection point for the Uzbek privatisation programme."*

### Pipeline: Uzbekistan Airways

Following the UzNIF listing, Uzbekistan Airways is progressing toward its own IPO (expected 2026), to be facilitated through UzNIF's existing capital markets infrastructure. The Airways IPO represents the next phase of the privatization programme.

1. State dominance in banking (SOCBs required recapitalization of 0.4–2.0% of GDP per year, 2017 onward).
2. Sizable preferential lending programmes crowding out market-rate allocation.
3. Underdeveloped non-bank financial institutions (no liquid secondary bond market; limited pension fund participation in equities).

## Topic 5: Central Bank of Uzbekistan — Policy Rate Trends

### Monetary Policy Framework

From **1 January 2020**, the Central Bank of Uzbekistan (CBU) formally replaced the refinancing rate with a **policy rate** as the main instrument of monetary operations and the primary target indicator for money market conditions. The policy rate directly determines monetary conditions required to anchor inflation toward the CBU's target of **5% per annum**.

The CBU operates an inflation-targeting framework. Policy meetings are held periodically; rate decisions are announced with accompanying analytical commentary. The CBU publishes quarterly Monetary Policy Reviews.

### Rate History (Reconstructed from Public Sources)

| Period | Policy Rate | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| End-2023 | **14.0%** | Elevated; CBU in restrictive stance |
| July 2024 | **13.5%** | Cut by 50 bps — easing cycle initiated |
| End-2024 | 13.5% | Held |
| 24 March 2025 | **14.0%** | Raised by 50 bps — reversal; inflation pressures |
| October–December 2025 | **14.0%** | Held; "Monetary Policy Guidelines 2026–2028" approved |
| March–June 2026 | **14.0%** | Held; three consecutive meetings unchanged |

### Relevance for Capital Market Liquidity

A high policy rate has direct implications for equity market liquidity:
1. **Cost of capital effect**: At 14%, risk-free returns on deposits and short-term instruments are high, reducing the relative attractiveness of equities for domestic investors.
2. **Discount rate effect**: High rates compress equity valuations by raising the denominator in DCF models, reducing incentives to hold equity.
3. **Institutional investor behavior**: Banks, facing high returns on lending, have less incentive to allocate to equity underwriting or market-making.
4. **Retail investor behavior**: Retail savers in a high-rate environment prefer bank deposits — consistent with Uzbekistan's unusually low retail deposit-to-GDP ratio (10% in 2021) and correspondingly low equity participation.

The CBU's restrictive stance since 2025 thus operates as a structural headwind to the equity market liquidity improvements that capital market reformers are simultaneously trying to promote.

## Topic 6: Why Liquidity Matters in Emerging Equity Markets

### Definition and Dimensions of Market Liquidity

Market liquidity refers to the ease and speed with which a security can be bought or sold without materially moving its price. In equity markets, liquidity encompasses multiple dimensions measured by different instruments:
- **Tightness**: bid-ask spread (transaction cost per unit traded)
- **Depth**: volume available at quoted prices without price impact
- **Breadth**: share of the market's securities that actively trade
- **Immediacy**: speed of trade execution at prevailing prices
- **Resilience**: how quickly prices recover after a large transaction

Composite liquidity indices — such as the UZLI — capture several of these dimensions simultaneously, as no single metric is a sufficient proxy.

### Foundational Academic Literature

**Levine, R. & Zervos, S. (1998). "Stock Markets, Banks, and Economic Growth." *American Economic Review*, 88(3), pp. 537–558.**
The founding empirical study of the relationship between stock market liquidity and economic growth. Across 47 countries from 1976–1993, initial stock market liquidity was a statistically significant and positive predictor of subsequent GDP per capita growth, capital accumulation, and productivity improvement — even after controlling for banking development, inflation, government spending, and political stability. The key finding: *liquidity in equity markets Granger-causes growth*, not merely correlates with it.
- SSRN: [https://papers.ssrn.com/sol3/papers.cfm?abstract_id=236909](https://papers.ssrn.com/sol3/papers.cfm?abstract_id=236909)
- NBER Replication Panel Evidence (Beck & Levine, 2002): [https://www.nber.org/system/files/working_papers/w9082/w9082.pdf](https://www.nber.org/system/files/working_papers/w9082/w9082.pdf)

*Note on robustness:* Zhu, Ash & Pollin (2004) in the *International Review of Applied Economics* (18:1) argued that the Levine-Zervos results are not robust to proper outlier controls. The debate does not undermine the direction of the relationship but does caution against mechanical policy conclusions.
- Critical appraisal: [https://papers.ssrn.com/sol3/papers.cfm?abstract_id=353366](https://papers.ssrn.com/sol3/papers.cfm?abstract_id=353366)

**Amihud, Y. (2002). "Illiquidity and Stock Returns: Cross-Section and Time-Series Effects." *Journal of Financial Markets*, 5(1), pp. 31–56.**
Introduced the Amihud illiquidity ratio (`|return| / dollar_volume`) as a proxy for price impact per unit of trading. Key findings: (i) expected market illiquidity is priced as a risk factor — higher illiquidity → higher required return; (ii) illiquidity premium is stronger for small-cap stocks; (iii) changes in illiquidity over time predict excess returns. This paper provides the theoretical basis for the Amihud component of the UZLI.
- ScienceDirect: [https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S1386418101000246](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S1386418101000246)
- Full text (UPenn): [https://www.cis.upenn.edu/~mkearns/finread/amihud.pdf](https://www.cis.upenn.edu/~mkearns/finread/amihud.pdf)

**Acharya, V.V. & Pedersen, L.H. (2005). "Asset Pricing with Liquidity Risk." *Journal of Financial Economics*, 77(2), pp. 375–410.**
Extended the CAPM to incorporate liquidity risk. The key result: a security's required return depends not only on its expected liquidity level but also on the covariance of its liquidity with market liquidity — i.e., securities that become illiquid precisely when the whole market is illiquid command a higher premium. In frontier markets, where liquidity is low on average AND highly correlated across all securities (as the UZLI breadth data suggests for Uzbekistan), both the level and the covariance effects are amplified.
- Full text (NYU Stern): [https://pages.stern.nyu.edu/~lpederse/papers/liquidity_risk.pdf](https://pages.stern.nyu.edu/~lpederse/papers/liquidity_risk.pdf)
- NBER Working Paper 10814: [https://www.nber.org/papers/w10814](https://www.nber.org/papers/w10814)

**Corwin, S.A. & Schultz, P. (2012). "A Simple Way to Estimate Bid-Ask Spreads from Daily High and Low Prices." *The Journal of Finance*, 67(2), pp. 719–760.**
Developed the high-low spread estimator used in the UZLI's spread_score component. The estimator exploits the asymmetry between the variance component (proportional to time interval) and the spread component (constant across intervals) of daily high-low ranges. Produces spread estimates from data easily available in any daily OHLCV dataset, without requiring intraday tick data.
- Wiley: [https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/abs/10.1111/j.1540-6261.2012.01729.x](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/abs/10.1111/j.1540-6261.2012.01729.x)
- SSRN Working Paper: [https://papers.ssrn.com/sol3/papers.cfm?abstract_id=1106193](https://papers.ssrn.com/sol3/papers.cfm?abstract_id=1106193)

### Why Illiquidity Is Particularly Damaging in Frontier Markets

**1. Cost of capital is elevated.**
Illiquid equity markets require investors to demand a higher return to compensate for exit risk. For firms, this means the cost of equity capital is structurally higher than in peer markets — reducing the attractiveness of equity financing relative to bank debt and suppressing the formation of new listings.

**2. Foreign investor access is constrained.**
International institutional investors (pension funds, sovereign wealth funds, index-tracking ETFs) typically require minimum liquidity thresholds before allocating capital to a market. FTSE's liquidity requirements for Secondary Emerging Market status are a direct expression of this. Uzbekistan's current liquidity profile — median 26 tickers trading per day out of 290 — does not yet support the liquidity criteria for systematic index inclusion.

**3. Price discovery is impaired.**
In thin markets, prices reflect idiosyncratic information about individual large trades rather than aggregated information about fundamentals. The UZSE's concentration of liquidity in a small subset of tickers means that official quoted prices for the ~200 dormant securities carry no reliable signal.

**4. Privatization revenues are depressed.**
For a government pursuing asset monetization through public markets — directly relevant to Uzbekistan's UzNIF and Uzbekistan Airways IPO programme — low secondary market liquidity reduces the valuation assigned by investors, since illiquidity is priced as a discount. Each basis point improvement in market liquidity translates directly into higher SOE valuations.

**5. Financial inclusion is limited.**
Liquid public equity markets allow households to participate in corporate wealth creation. In Uzbekistan, the historical privatization wave created over 1.2 million nominal shareholders, but dormant secondary markets meant this represented paper rather than liquid wealth.

### IMF and World Bank Position

The IMF's Global Financial Stability Report (April 2020) chapter on Emerging and Frontier Markets identified low secondary market liquidity as a primary structural vulnerability, noting that frontier markets face amplified capital flow volatility precisely because thin liquidity transforms moderate portfolio outflows into outsized price dislocations.

The World Bank's financial sector development framework consistently identifies liquid capital markets as a prerequisite — not a byproduct — of financial deepening: without secondary market liquidity, primary markets for corporate debt and equity cannot develop, restricting the financing mix available to firms and concentrating intermediation risk in the banking system.
